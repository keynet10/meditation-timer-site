What is Brown Noise?

To understand brown noise, you have to understand the color spectrum of sound. White noise (like television static) contains all frequencies across the spectrum at equal intensity. For many people with ADHD, the high-pitch hiss of white noise is actually overstimulating and grating.

Brown noise, on the other hand, rolls off those harsh, high frequencies. It emphasizes the lower frequencies, creating a deep, resonant rumble. It sounds like a heavy rainstorm, a rushing river, or the low hum of an airplane cabin. It is powerful, yet completely unobtrusive.

Why Does Brown Noise Help with ADHD Focus?

The science behind why brown noise works for neurodivergent brains usually comes down to two main factors:

1. Auditory Masking

People with ADHD often have a lower threshold for external distractions. A dog barking down the street or a coworker typing can completely derail a train of thought. Brown noise acts as an acoustic blanket, masking these unpredictable, sharp sounds so your brain doesn't have to process them.

2. Optimal Arousal Theory

ADHD brains are notoriously under-stimulated, which is why they constantly seek out new inputs (leading to distraction). Providing a steady, predictable stream of low-frequency audio gives the brain just enough background stimulation to satisfy it, allowing the executive functions to focus purely on the task at hand without feeling "bored."

How to Build a Brown Noise Focus Routine

Simply turning on brown noise isn't always enough. To maximize your deep work, pair it with structured timing:

  • Use intervals: The Pomodoro technique (working for 25 minutes, resting for 5) pairs perfectly with ambient audio.
  • Remove visual clutter: Audio masking works best when paired with visual simplicity. Put your phone face down or use an app with a pitch-black interface to stop the screen from lighting up and distracting you.
  • Avoid "loop clicks": Many free YouTube videos or cheap apps have a noticeable pause or "click" when the 10-minute audio file loops. This instantly breaks focus. Always use a tool with a true, gapless audio engine.
